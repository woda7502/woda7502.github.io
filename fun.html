M51 — The Whirlpool Galaxy

The Whirlpool Galaxy, M51, is a spiral galaxy located 31 million light-years away. It highlights the attributes of a typical spiral galaxy.

M104 — The Sombrero Galaxy

M104 is located 28 million light-years away in the constellation Virgo, and with a mass equal to 800 billion suns, it is one of the most massive objects in the Virgo galaxy cluster.

NGC 6960 — The Western Veil Nebula

NGC 6960, also known as the Witch's Broom Nebula or the Western Veil Nebula, is a supernova remnant in the constellation of Cygnus. It is the shattered remnants of a star, more than 20 times the mass of the Sun, that exploded as a supernova roughly 8,000 to 15,000 years ago.

M42 — The Orion Nebula

M42, the Orion Nebula, is a turbulent, nearby star-forming region and a "stellar nursery" where thousands of stars are born, including the Trapezium cluster in its core.

NGC 2403

NGC 2403 is a spiral galaxy located in the Camelopardalis constellation roughly 11 million light-years from Earth, known for its numerous star-forming regions (H II regions), which appear as pinkish-glowing clouds.

M57 — The Ring Nebula

M57, the Ring Nebula, is a well-known planetary nebula located in the constellation Lyra, formed by the dying gases of a Sun-like star.

M27 — The Dumbbell Nebula

M27, known as the Dumbbell Nebula, is a planetary nebula in the Vulpecula constellation, formed when a Sun-like star died and expelled its outer layers, creating a colorful, ionized gas shell around a white dwarf.

M13 — The Hercules Cluster

M13 is a dense, 11.65-billion-year-old globular star cluster in the constellation Hercules, about 25,000 light-years from Earth, containing over 100,000 stars within a 150-light-year-wide ball.

The Beehive Cluster & Mars

This image contains the Beehive Cluster or Praesepe, a nearby open star cluster located in the constellation of Cancer, along with the planet Mars — the bright orange object in the top left. This appearance is due to the long exposure needed to capture both the planet and the cluster.

The Northern Lights — Gannon Storm

This image was taken during the May 10th, 2024 G5 geomagnetic solar storm, the first to hit in over two decades, given the name "The Gannon Storm" in memory of leading space weather physicist Jennifer Gannon.
